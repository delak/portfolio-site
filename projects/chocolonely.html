cocoa farmers with Design Thinking intro:

The Chocolonely Foundation (the non-profit arm of Tony’s Chocolonely) wanted to know whether (or to what extent) Design Thinking could be used to empower people living in the rural cocoa-farming communities that they sourced their cocoa from, we ran a year-long program (dubbed the ‘Designathon’).

We defined 8 core skills and outcomes. Seeing the participants take the lead on the discussion of what next for the Do Good club was a great moment for us. The fact that they are owning the process and have started making plans about leadership, contributions, and problem solving in the community is a great start and we look forward to hearing about the impact that the club makes.

brief: |
  • Lead UX Expert
  • Ethnographic research with more than 100 farmers over 2 years
  • Content design for low-literate users
  • Mobile app design for customer registration by sales agents

1Field Research
Over the course of the project we observed and conducted interviews with farmers in 6 out of the 10 regions of Ghana. Some of the stories we heard centered around a lack of pride, lack of negotiation power with traders; low literacy and heavy post-harvest losses.
2Initial research synthesis
Based on an initial 6-weeks of research and stakeholder engagement, we developed archetypes and a Minimum Viable Product for further testing.
3Spend calendar
A fun and interactive tool to visualize and discuss farmers’ spending on phone credit.
4Content testing
A simple paper and cardboard prototype of a feature phone allowed us to test content quickly and easily.

This farmer archetype is reactive to external circumstances and often feels cheated. Doesn’t feel empowered to change their situation. Struggles to make ends meet.

Escapist
Tech Literacy
Business Sense

Starts a side business to complement their farming activities, especially after experiencing difficulties (such as low or inconsistent income or physical limitations).

Acceptor

Business Sense

Needs assistance maintaining a profitable farm. Trusts information received from partners; follows best practices. Family’s well-being is most important to them.

Competent Optimist
Tech Literacy

Runs a productive farming business; keeps books and stays up to date about new farming developments. Is the first one to try new methods and shares their results.

5Phone charm
The phone charm served to: increase the brand visibility of Farmers’ Club; improve farmer pride; and provide a convenient place for farmers to reference their own phone numbers — a major pain point.
6SMS prefixes
Even though most farmers couldn't read, we found that they were often quite adept at recognizing simple codes. System messages were prefixed with alphanumeric codes such as R02 ("Renew with 2 cedis"), so they wouldn't even have to be read.

Sales Agent tools

In addition to addressing the needs of the farmers themselves, we also had to address the difficult challenge of how to distribute the product to rural communities all around Ghana. We helped to implement a Sales Agent and Ambassador model, which included specially-designed tools and training.

7Sales agent app
Devised as a way of giving agents a set of digital tools to help onboard users and to streamline the registration process for new customers.
8Role playing
Agricultural extension agents practice how to explain the service to farmers by role playing.
9Cheat sheet
We found that technological illiteracy was underpinned by one dominant factor: a lack of confidence. People didn't want to “play’ with their phones for fear of spoiling them. We designed the cheat sheet — a strip of paper imprinted with the function keys of a typical feature phone — to empower farmers to check and delete messages.
10Voice SMS
Voice-recorded farm tips were trialled in multiple languages.
